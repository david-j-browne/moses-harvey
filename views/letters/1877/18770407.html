April 7, 1877 Saturday

     In an able and lucid speech the Hon. J.J. Rogerson, Receiver-General, submitted his annual statement of the affairs of the colony for the past year to the House of Assembly.  He informed the House that the total revenue for the year 1876 amounted to $855 228.  The main portion of this amount was derived from customs' duties, which had yielded $807 761.  The postal revenue was $15 000;  that from Crown lands $4 793; from licenses, $6 616, the remainder being made up from various sources.  Lighthouse dues amounted to $29 112, but nearly the whole sum is absorbed in the maintenance of lighthouses and harbor lights, now numbering 25.  The sum of $11 131 had been paid as bouties on the construction of one hundred vessels of 4 070 tons.  The average rate of taxation per capita for last year was $5.29 as compared with $5.77 in 1870.  "This," said the Receiver-General, "is probably about the lowest rate of taxation on this side of the Atlantic, showing that the increase of population gives a greater area and reduces the per capita expenditure upon account of revenue.

     The debenture debt of the Colony amounts to $ 1 319 390, the interest being $65 710.  On this, the Receiver-General remarked, "The pro rata liability of the country, on account of the funded public debt, is at present $8.62 and 1/2 per head, based on the census of 1874.  The large outlay on account of our public institutions, our lighthouse establishments, as well as the opening up of the country by roads, and other useful and necessary public works, was well represented in this obligation.

     To meet the expenditure for the current year, the Receiver-General estimates that a revenue of $858 000 will be required.  The annual charges on the revenue he states as follows:-

Steam and Postal Service ---------- $145 180

Education -------------------------   88 260

     Roads and Bridges -----------------   90 000

     Court Houses and Gaols ------------    7 000

     Police and Magisterial Department -   57 788

     Relief of Poor --------------------  123 377

     Pensions --------------------------   13 717

     Public Debt Interest --------------   67 775

     Geological Survey -----------------    5 000

     Special Vote for Public Works -----   20 000

Besides Judicial and other Branches of the Civil Service.

     To encourage the bank fishery, $2 000 are to be expended; to carry out scientific explorations at Northern Labrador $2000; for the education of deaf-mutes $500.  The London Board of Trade had authorized the construction of a new steam boiler for the Cape Race fog whistle; and also agreed to pay 300lbs. stg. for the construction of a road from Cape Race lighthouse to Clam Cove.

     Our Receiver-General has long been an ardent advocate for the encourage of home industries, so as to provide employment for our population.  A boot and shoe factory has been established here which employs 160 hands, and distributes a large amount in wages.  There is no reason why the immense quantity of nets, twines, cordage, etc, required by our fishermen should not be manufactured here, but at present we have but one small factory which employs few hands.  Hemp, flax, raw cotton and wool, imported into the colony for manufacturing purposes, are admitted duty free, as well as all machinery for that purpose; and in addition, the Receiver-General proposes to pay a premium of 5 percent on the original cost at port of shipment, on all material for the manufacture of nets, lines, cordage, etc.  "If", said the Receiver-General, "we can only share in a manufacture of a portion of the fishing gear and coarse woolen apparel now imported into the colony, it will give employment to many idle hands now among us, as the following figures, giving the value of our importations in 1876 will demonstrate:-

           Cordage...................$122,495

           Fishing Tackle............ 125,025

           Oakum.....................   6,156

     He further proposes to lower the duty on tallow from 13 to 5 percent, so as to give opportunity for the establishment of soap and candle factories, of which we have none.  A tobacco factory has just been opened by Mr. Archibald, of Halifax, which will employ a large number of hands.  The raw lwaf is to be admitted on four months bond for duty, instead of cash as is charged on the manufactured article.  The duty on leaf tobacco is 7 cents per pound.  Such are the leading points in the Receiver-General's statement which allows that the revenue is in a healthy state, and that the condition of the colony is decidedly prosperous.  Our revenue is equal to the present very liberal expenditure, and for any important undertaking, such as a railroad, we could without inconvenience, bear an increase of taxation.  The total cost of the Railway Survey has been about $45,000.  The expenditure was a wise one, as it will be the first step toward a great public work which will fairly start the colony on a path of progress.  The day, I trust, is not distant when England, Canada and this colony will unite in a construction of a railway across this island - a work of international importance, as shortening the route between two continents, and materially reducing its dangers.  It has reported that there has been lately a communication from an eminent English railway contractor on the subject of the construction of our railway, but the nature of his propositions has not yet been made public.  The most liberal terms would undoubtedly be granted by our Government to a company who would undertake its construction.  no doubt the present depression in trade is favourable to the initiation of the enterprise.

     On the 10th of March our steam fleet, numbering in all twenty-three steamers, took their departure for the ice fileds.  They had on board about 4,00 stalwart men - the elite of our seal hunters.  They have started jnder the most favourable circumstances, the coast being entirely clear of ice, so that no impediment existed to retard the steamers in reaching the "seal meadows".  Such a condition of things is almost unknown during the last thirty years, as usually we have either a complete ice blockade at the beginning of March, or vast ice fields at no great distance from the shore.  Everything being thus favourable for the hunt, we have "great expectations" regarding the results.  The hunters getting early within reach of their prey, have a much better chance of finding their quarry than in former years.  It was with peculiar fervour that our sealers, before starting, drank their favourite toasts this season:- "Death to our best friends," and "may we soon see bloody decks," as they expect soon to realize these bloodthirsty expectations.  The Arctic and Aurora, Dundee steamers, have taken between them 500 of our stout fellows, who, with the ample allowances of good food and the comfortable quarters provided for them will find themselves in paradisaical condition, compared with those on board our Newfoundland steamers, where the fare is much more meagre, and the men have to rough it considerably.  The Scotch standard of comfort for the men is much higher than that of this country, and it is to be hoped that this will have the effect of bringing ours up to a higher level.  In truth, there is much room for improvement.  In addition to the steamers, 36 sailing vessels have taken their departure from various ports carrying 2,658 men; so that altogether, 6,658 men are engaged in the slaughter of the youthful "white coats."  In all probability, the havoc will be great; but as yet we have no news from hunting grounds.  Owing to the long continuance of westerly winds the coast is entirely free from ice as far as Cape John.  In fact, the "oldest inhabitant" does not remember such a mild winter as the present.  It reminds one of an English winter.  Of course this is owing mainly to the want of our usual ice-belt, brought down on the bosom of the Arctic current.

     Communication by sea with the mining region is cut off in winter, and the overland mail, for some reason, has proved a failure this year.  We have no later dates from Bett's Cove and Tilt Cove Mines than Fecbruary 9th.  At the former mine, an unfortunate accident occurred resulting in the deaths of five children and one man.  It appears some of the houses are built directly under a cliff on the summit of which a great mass of snow accumulated early in the winter.  Owing probably to the mild weather, and a high wind which prevailled at the time, the snow became detached and slid down, burying two houses under ten feet of snow.  The utmost efforts were made to dig out the inmates, thirty-four in number; but sad to relate five children were taken out dead, and one man was so much injured that he died soon after.  All the others were rescued unhurt.  Mining operations are carried on with great vigour at Bett's Cove, and six hundred men are employed on the works.  At Tilt Cove too, mining continues to yield excellent returns.

     A despatch from Lord Carnarvon, in reference to the appointment of magistrates on the so-called "French Shore", has been made public.  Permission is granted for such appointments, and for the collection of revenue there, but Lord carnarvon stipulates that the selection and appointment should be left to the Secretary of state, and that the colony pay each 500 pounds sterling per annum. The reason assigned for this is that these magistrates having to discharge duties involving the interpretation and observance of treaties for which the imperial Government is responsible, must be selected by the imperial authorities, but we are to have the honour of paying their rather handsome salaries.  Now, we decidedly object to this arrangement.  If we pay, we must select the men.  It is likely a compromise will be accepted, and that we shall be allowed to appoint, subject to the approval of the Secretary of the Colonies.  If the territory is ours, on what principle do the Imperial authorities require to hold in their hands the appointment of magistrates?  It is another instance of the weak policy pursued for generations by British statesmen in dealing with the preposterous claims of the French.  They will speedily find it unprofitable to fish along a shore already settled.

     Since the foregoing was written, the steamer Greenland arrived from the ice with 27,000 seals, all in prime condition.  They are fully worth $81,000.  She was but sixteen days out.  Where is the goldmine to compare with this?  She got her seals far north - off Labrador - and saw only five sailing vessels in the same vicinity, all loaded.  Saw no steamers.  Seals enough where she was to load the Great Eastern three times.  The news is considered favourable as far as it goes.
