January 27 1877

Our Newfoundland letter  January 19

The legislature is summoned to meet for the dispatch of business on the first Thursday in February. It is not anticipated that any measures of great importance will be introduced by Government during the session, or such as will involve any great changes in the present even tenor of our way. Internal reforms and an adaption of existing laws to the encouragement of agricultural, lumbering and mining enterprises are urgently needed. When, at the present moment, no man can purchase for farming purposes more than 100 acres of land, no matter what may be his capital or the extent of his views, and when no large grant then 200 acres will be given to any one for lumbering, and when even in the very best agricultural and lumbering district—that of the Gander—no one can obtain a grant of a single acre of land for any purpose, it is evident that our laws require wholesale revision. To this it is to be hoped our Government will address themselves at once, and at least remove obstacles out of the way of industrial enterprises. A new land and forest act is urgently required. It is also quite time that the telegraph wire was extended to the mining region, where population is rapidly increasing, more especially as during winter, communication by sea with the capital is cut off, and mail communication is maintained at long intervals by MicMac Indians across a roadless country. It is very probable that a measure for telegraphic extension in this direction, will be introduced at an early period of the session. The construction of an ordinary road, along the route of the projected railway, is also spoken of. Such a road would open up the country and lead to the settlement of districts favorable for agriculture, and would greatly facilitate the construction of a railway along the same track, when the time arrives for this important undertaking. Our Government are now in a good position for initiating such works of public utility. They have an overflowing revenue, amounting this year to $810,000, the highest figure yet reached, and indicating beyond mistake the prosperous condition of the colony, in the midst of a general depression of trade. The influence of the hard times is scarcely felt here. Of course this prosperity makes governing easy work. The people are contented; merchants and traders at the end of the year have reason to be satisfied with the condition of their ledgers, and the working classes are, as a rule, in comfortable circumstances. We may anticipate, therefore, that the popularity of our Government will continue and increase, under such favorable conditions, and that they will be able to push forward public works of importance.

The S.S. George Cromwell

Serious apprehensions regarding the steamer George Cromwell of the Cromwell line, plying between New York and St. John’s are entertained. She left Halifax for St. John’s on the 3rd of January, and has not simce been heard of. There were on board the crew, and, as far as known at present, only five passengers. It may be that owing to the failure of her machinery, she is lying in a disable condition, or had been fallen in with by some other vessel; but now that she is 10 days over due, matters look gloomy. Under canvas the vessel could make little way, and as striong westerly winds have prevailed, she may have been obliged to run eastward, and may be heard of from the otherside of the Atlantic. The Cromwell steamers have been remarkably successful, made excellent runs all summer, and kept time quite as well as the Allan steamers, and this without a single mishap. Passengers, too, spoke well of the accommodation on board. It was a mistake, however, to continue running after winter had fairly set in, as the build of these steamers is not such as to enable them to contend with the stormy weather in these boisterous regions. Let us hope that the Cromwell will soon be heard of as all right.

Disasters at sea

The late heavy gale have been attended with serious loss of life around these shores. A month ago the Fire? Fly left Bonne Bay for St. John’s, and has not since been herad of. But faint hopes are now entertained of her safety. No less than 28 souls were on board. The schooner William left St. John’s on the 16th December for Twillingate, and has not yet arrived; great fears are entertained that she has been driven away to sea, along with five other small coasters, during the heavy gale of December 17th. There is now but little hope that any of these ill-fated vessels will ever be heard of.

Lost of the snow ???
On the morning of the 15th Constable Ringwood, of Bay Roberts, was found frozen to death on Bareweed Hill. The unfortunate man had been to Port-au-Grave on duty, and on his return home was overtaken by a heavy snow-storm, lost his way, and at length sank down exhausted on the spot where his body was found. It appears he suffered from an affliction of the eyes, and this, added to the thick weather, sufficeintlty accounts for the melancholy event. He has been on the constabulary for fifteen years and bore an excellent character.
